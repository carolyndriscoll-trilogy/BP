    - Source 5: Kurt Fischer Overview (Human Dev), Chat******
      - DOK 1 - Facts
        - Kurt Fischer's Research: A Comprehensive Analysis
        - Background and Academic Journey
        - Kurt W. Fischer (1943-2020) was a pioneering developmental psychologist who fundamentally transformed our understanding of human cognitive development. As the Charles Bigelow Professor of Education at Harvard Graduate School of Education and founder of the Mind, Brain, and Education program, Fischer bridged neuroscience, psychology, and education in groundbreaking ways.
        - Dynamic Skill Theory: Core Framework
        - Fundamental Principles
        - Fischer's Dynamic Skill Theory revolutionized developmental psychology by proposing that cognitive development is:
        - 1. **Non-linear and Dynamic**: Unlike Piaget's stage theory, Fischer demonstrated that development follows complex pathways with variations, regressions, and multiple trajectories
        - 2. **Context-Dependent**: Skills emerge and vary based on environmental support, emotional state, and specific domains
        - 3. **Hierarchically Organized**: Skills build upon each other in increasingly complex tiers
        - The Skill Scale Architecture
        - Fischer identified **13 developmental levels** organized into four tiers:
        - **Tier 1: Reflexes (birth - 4 months)**
        - Single reflexes
        - Reflex mappings
        - Reflex systems
        - **Tier 2: Sensorimotor Actions (4 months - 2 years)**
        - Single actions
        - Action mappings
        - Action systems
        - **Tier 3: Representations (2 years - 10-12 years)**
        - Single representations
        - Representational mappings
        - Representational systems
        - **Tier 4: Abstractions (10-12 years - adulthood)**
        - Single abstractions
        - Abstract mappings
    - Source 6: Random Tangents of Human Dev, Chat*****
      - DOK 1 - Facts
        - 1. Developmental Psychology
          - Core Idea:
            - Developmental psychology studies how people change across their lifespan—cognitively, emotionally, socially, and behaviorally.
          - Key Dimensions:
            - Cognitive development: How thinking and understanding evolve (e.g., Piaget’s stages)
            - Emotional development: How emotional awareness, regulation, and empathy form
            - Social development: How we form relationships and navigate society
            - Moral development: How we understand right and wrong
          - Why it matters:
            - Many adult struggles (e.g., insecurity, perfectionism, avoidance) are rooted in how certain developmental needs were or weren’t met.
          - Modern focus includes:
            - Critical periods (e.g., early childhood for language or emotional attachment)
            - Neuroplasticity and sensitive windows
            - Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACEs)
        - 2. Attachment Theory
          - Founder: John Bowlby, later expanded by Mary Ainsworth
          - Core Idea:
            - The bond between a child and their primary caregiver forms a blueprint for how the child will relate to themselves and others throughout life.
          - Attachment Styles:
            - Secure: Trusts others, feels worthy of love
            - Anxious: Craves closeness, fears abandonment
            - Avoidant: Avoids intimacy, feels they must be self-reliant
            - Disorganized: Fearful, often a result of trauma or abuse
          - Long-term impacts:
            - Shapes romantic relationships, self-esteem, emotional regulation

    - Source 2: Basic Intrinsic Motivation & Human Dev, Ephor
      - DOK 1 - Facts
        - Intrinsic Motivation
          - Core Principles
            - Autonomy – Feeling in control of choices
            - Competence – Feeling capable and growing
            - Relatedness – Feeling connected and supported
          - Let them choose - within structure
            - Edward Deci’s research (1995) found that rewards and control reduce intrinsic motivation, while autonomy boosts it.
            - Give meaningful choices (e.g., “Do you want to read now or after snack?”)
            - Let them pick the how, even if you set the what (“You need to write one page. Want to do it with a pencil, on a whiteboard, or dictation?”)
            - **AUTONOMY
          - Shift from praise to process feedback
            - Carol Dweck showed that praising effort, strategy, and persistence builds a growth mindset, which fuels intrinsic motivation.
            - Avoid: “You’re so smart!”
            - Do: “I saw you worked hard even when it got tough—how’d you push through?”
          - Model curiosity and internal drive
            - Angela Duckworth emphasizes “modeling grit”—children internalize what they see in emotionally significant adults.
            - Narrate your interests: “I’m trying to figure out why this tool works. It’s tricky but fun.”
            - Let them see you fail and try again: “This recipe flopped—back to the drawing board!”
          - Design for small wins and competence
            - Daniel Willingham (Why Don’t Students Like School?): motivation rises when a child sees consistent progress—not when things are too easy or too hard.
            - Break challenges into bite-sized goals they can reach
            - Scaffold: support just enough to keep them in the “zone of proximal development” (Vygotsky)
          - Flow and fun, connect learning to real purpose
            - Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi (Flow): Deep motivation and satisfaction come from immersion in meaningful challenges.
            - Tie tasks to identity and value: “You love animals—what could you invent to help them?”
            - Involve them in real-world projects (e.g., selling crafts, writing a blog, organizing a mini science fair)
            -  
          - Remove the overjustification trap
            - Deci & Ryan’s experiments show that external rewards can undermine motivation for tasks a child previously enjoyed.
            - Avoid: Paying or excessively praising them for reading, drawing, or creative play.
            - Do: Celebrate engagement, not outcome: “You were so into that drawing. What were you thinking while doing it?”
          - Big do nots
            - Bribing with rewards (“Do X, get Y”)
            - Shaming or comparing (“Why can’t you be like your sister?”)
            - Micromanaging (removes autonomy)
            - Focusing only on results (grades, wins, outcomes)
        - Human Development Stages --> Check Trauma Node
          - Infancy (0–2 years)
            - Physical: Rapid brain growth, sensory development, motor skills (sitting, crawling, walking).
            - Cognitive (Piaget - Sensorimotor): Learning through sensory input and motor actions; object permanence develops.
            - Psychosocial (Erikson - Trust vs. Mistrust): Key task is forming a secure attachment.
            - Consistent care = trust; neglect = mistrust.
            - Outcome: Security/optimism vs. fear/suspicion.
          - Early Childhood (2–6 years)
            - Physical: Fine motor skills, rapid language acquisition.
            - Cognitive (Piaget - Preoperational): Egocentrism, symbolic play, limited logic.
            - Psychosocial (Erikson - Autonomy vs. Shame / Initiative vs. Guilt): Developing independence and a sense of purpose.
          - Middle Childhood (6–12 years)
            - Physical: Slower, steady growth; mastery of coordination.
            - Cognitive (Piaget - Concrete Operational): Logical thinking about concrete events, understanding of conservation and rules.
            - Psychosocial (Erikson - Industry vs. Inferiority): Developing competence and self-worth via tasks and peer comparison.
    - Source 3: Self-Determination Theory, Gemini
      - DOK 1 - Facts
        - SDT - Edward Deci and Richard Ryan. Says that if our universal needs are met, it will promote optimal functioning, growth, and well-being
        - 3 Fundamental Needs
          - Autonomy: the feeling that one has control over their life and is the origin of their own behavior. Free will and choice.
            - Punnet square of free will (2 vars: do have free will and belief of it)
            - OWNERSHIP in a sense
            - Looks like: choices within appropriate limits like choosing which book to read, having a say in their academic path, or making career choices that align with their values / agency over relationships 
          - Competence: feeling of being effective in one’s interactions with the environment and experiencing opportunities to exercise and express one’s capacities. Feeling capable and skilled
            - infant = crawling or reaching for objects
            - school-aged child = successfully completing a math problem or learning to ride a bike
            - adults = learning a new skill, excelling at work
          - Relatedness: feeling of being connected to others, cared for, belonging to a group or community, secure and understood in relationships
            - infant = secure attachment with a primary caregiver 
            - child = close friendships and feeling accepted
            - adolescents = sense of belonging
            - adults = supportive relationships, family bonds, and broader larger community connections
        - SDT in Human Development
          - Promoting Optimal Development
            - When environments consistently support and individual’s needs for the 3 needs, that individual is more likely to thrive, be intrinsically motivated, and achieve well-being
            - Infancy = foundation of trust and autonomy. Letting kid choose between two snacks, not overly controlling parents who dictate every move. Challenging your baby to let them build confidence in their abilities not making it super easy. Being consistently warm, response, and caring.
            - Adolescence (Identity and Mastery) = teacher giving options, adolescent choosing extracurricular activities, providing challenging but achievable tasks, learning how to apply skills. Getting positive constructive and specific feedback. Fostering a sense of community, collaboration, supportive, understanding
            - Adulthood (Generativity and Self-Actualization) = having flexibility in work schedule, option to pursue personal projects, making life choices, opportunities for feedback to improve, professional development, recognition for achievements, continuing to learn new skills or hobbies. maintaining strong social networks, nurturing close friendships, etc.
            - THESE ALL ENCOMPASS THE 3 NEEDS
          - Impact of Thwarted Needs
            - When environment fails to provide opportunities for building autonomy, competence, and relatedness, or actively undermines them, individual can have PROBLEMS
            - Motivation & Engagement: if you feel controlled, dumb (incompetent), and disconnected, you will likely become NOT motivated
            - Psychological Distress
              - Low Autonomy: can lead to anxiety, depression, feelings of helplessness, or rebellious behavior as a desperate attempt to assert control
              - Low Competence: can lead to low self-esteem, learned helplessness, a fear of failure, or avoidance of challenge —> arrogance because of illusion of competence
              - Low Relatedness: can lead to lonelinesss, social anxiety, depression, or difficulty forming healthy attachments
            - Maladaptive Behaviors: a teen who feels controlled at home and incompetent at school mike seek belonging and a sense of power through peer groups involved in risky behaviors. An adult experiencing the same at work might resort to passive-aggressive behaviors or withdrawal
          - Developmental Transitions
            - Adolescence: intensely focused on identity formations
              - Autonomy: need to make their own choices, express individuality, separate from parental control
              - Relatedness: intense need for peer acceptance and belonging because of the latter
              - Competence: desire to feel capable in social, academic, and physical domains
          - Application
            - It’s a prescriptive framework: tells us what environments and interactions are most conducive to human flourishing
            - It’s an explanatory framework: it helps us understand why certain developmental outcomes occur based on whether basic psychological needs are met or thwarted
            - It’s a guide for intervention: in parenting, education, therapy, workplace, etc. SDT can be consciously applied to foster self-motivation, well-being, and healthy development
