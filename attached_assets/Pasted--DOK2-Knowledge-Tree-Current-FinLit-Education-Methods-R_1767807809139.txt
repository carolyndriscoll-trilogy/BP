      - Gamified Classroom
        - HandsOnBanking: money management simulation teaching important financial education concepts such as budgeting, comparison shopping, evaluating needs, wants, and trade offs here and here
          - Program is a virtual classroom simulation, not a self-contained online game or app. It's designed for live, guided use over video conferencing or in-person class, using PDFs and a slide presentation
            - Instructor-led: The simulation is run by a teacher or facilitator who guides students through a PowerPoint-style presentation with embedded decision points ("stations")
            - Materials-Based - Students receive:
              - A participant profile (emailed individually)
              - A budget worksheet (fillable PDF or printable)
              - A Budgeting Tips handout
            - Presentation-Driven: The core activity takes place via a slide-based presentation shared through screen share (Zoom, Google Meet, etc.) or projected in a classroom
            - Manual Input: Students manually update their budget worksheets based on the decisions they make at each station
            - No centralized online platform: There is no login portal, user dashboard, or real-time automated scoring system. All interactions rely on the student’s engagement with emailed documents and live facilitation
          - Elements of program:
            - Participant Profiles: Each student receives a unique profile detailing career, income, education, family status, and financial obligations (like student loans)
            - Budget Worksheet: A running ledger where students track spending, income, and remaining balance throughout the simulation
            - 13 Station Slides: Students visit virtual “stations” (housing, transportation, childcare, insurance, groceries) where they choose among lifestyle options with different costs
            - Chance Cards: Inject unexpected events (hail damage, birthday gifts) to simulate real-life financial uncertainty
            - Retirement & Loan Planning: Automatically built into the worksheet to reinforce the importance of long-term financial thinking
          - Implementation Steps:
            - Before the Session
              - Distribute unique student profiles and budgeting worksheets
              - Send Budgeting tips handout
              - Prepare the presentation slides and simulation flow (~1 hour)
            - During the Simulation
              - Guide students through each station and have them record their choices and expenses
              - Introduce chance cards at appropriate moments
              - Encourage students to stay within budget (if they run out of money, they must revisit choices)
            - Debrief
              - Discuss takeaways, budgeting strategies, and how education and family affected financial outcomes
              - Reflect on the real-world implications of financial choices
            - Feedback
              - Collect feedback via the provided form to improve future sessions
        - Charles Schwab Lesson: lesson plan similar to MCE/JA Finance park, game of life (category 1 - basic simulation)
          - Classroom rearranged to resemble a life-size game of life, use ledgers to track transactions, roll dice to move around room, and have time to reflect following the lesson
          - Likely not as comprehensive/immersive/interactive as JA or MCE (JA = superior form of classroom modification + IRL simulation)

          - Best for middle school and older, simulation where the player is running out of money. Each time a student makes a decision, the program explains the consequences of that choice. The game ends when the player runs out of money.
        - GiveGet: FinLit game used to teach budgeting, saving, and borrowing
          - Parent company: Finance411
          - Format: Digital board game where students move around the board, making decisions about money. The game focuses on learning basic financial concepts and practicing good money behaviors.
          - Gameplay Core: The fundamental mechanic revolves around making decisions to "Give" money (for essential and unexpected expenses, savings, or investments) to potentially "Get" money back.
          - Learning Focus:
            - Budgeting: Students must understand essential and unexpected expenses by establishing an emergency fund.
            - Investment Categories: The game uses four key spaces (called the Four Corners) to represent different financial concepts:
              - Piggy: Represents a guaranteed savings account (e.g., checking accounts, CDs).
              - Chart: Represents a non-guaranteed investment account (e.g., individual stocks, index funds).
              - Ticket: Represents speculation or gambling (e.g., lottery, sports betting).
              - House: Represents asset ownership (e.g., primary residence, business venture).
            - Borrowing: Represented by the "Pillars" space, illustrating how a loan can be positive if there is a plan for repayment.
          - Goal: A student wins by either causing their opponent to go bankrupt or by being the first player to achieve a net worth of at least $100. The game is also used for national competitions with scholarship opportunities.

    - [3] Sandbox Economy Games
      - The Sims
        - Release Year: 2000 (Original), 2014 (The Sims 4)
        - Consistent Players: ~8–10 million Monthly Active Users (estimated across all platforms; Steam data alone shows ~30k concurrent daily, but the majority play on EA App/Console).
        - The Game Loop:
          - Needs Management: Maintain hunger, hygiene, energy, and social meters.
          - Labor: Go to work to earn Simoleons
          - Upgrade: Buy better furniture/house upgrades which drain "Needs" slower (e.g., a better bed restores energy faster).
          - Repeat: With more free time (from efficient furniture), focus on skills/hobbies.
        - Dominant Drives: CD4 (Ownership) and CD3 (Creativity)
          - The White Hat (Positive) Loop:
            - CD4: Ownership & Possession (The Anchor): This is the strongest drive. The player essentially "owns" the humans. The more you play, the more furniture, skills, and career promotions your Sims acquire. The feeling of "I built this house" is paramount.
            - CD3: Empowerment of Creativity & Feedback: You have infinite choices in how to build a house or structure a life. The feedback is instant: buy a stove -> Sim eats better -> Sim is happy.
            - CD2: Development & Accomplishment: Watching the career bar progress from "Mailroom Clerk" to "CEO."
          - The Black Hat (Negative/Urgency) Loop:
            - CD8: Loss & Avoidance: If you don't pay bills, the Repoman comes (Loss of assets). If you don't eat, you die (Loss of progress).
          - Builder's Takeaway: The addiction comes from the CD4/CD8 balance. The fear of losing what you "Own" (CD4) drives the labor required to avoid the "Loss" (CD8).
        - Engagement Score: High Retention / Low Intensity. Players often "binge" this game for weeks and then drop it for months.
        - Builder’s Note: The genius of The Sims is Maslow’s Hierarchy of Needs as a game mechanic. The player starts desperate for basics (food/toilet) and progressively unlocks the freedom to pursue "Self Actualization" (painting, astronomy, romance).
          - Lesson: Make "survival" annoying but "thriving" rewarding - similar to real life
      - Animal Crossing
        - Release Year: 2020 (Series started 2001)
        - Consistent Players: Peaked at ~40M copies sold. Currently sustains a lower but cult-like daily user base (estimated ~100k–200k daily concurrents globally) due to seasonal events.
        - The Game Loop:
          - Daily Check-in: Check turnip prices, dig up fossils, shake trees.
          - Collection: Catch bugs/fish to donate to the museum or sell for Bells.
          - Investment: Pay off loan to expand house or build island infrastructure.
          - Wait: Real-time clock means you must wait until "tomorrow" for buildings to finish.
        - Dominant Drives: CD6 (Scarcity) and CD5 (Social)
          - The White Hat (Positive) Loop:
            - CD4: Ownership & Possession: Collecting every fossil, bug, and fish to fill the museum. It is a completionist’s dream.
            - CD5: Social Influence & Relatedness: The "Turnip Economy" forces players to talk outside the game. "Hey, my island is buying turnips for 500 bells!" triggers massive social coordination.
          - The Black Hat (Negative/Urgency) Loop:
            - CD6: Scarcity & Impatience (The Hook): This is the game's masterstroke. You cannot speed up time (without cheating). If a shop closes at 10 PM, it is closed. If you miss a seasonal fish in March, you wait until next year. This creates artificial value through scarcity.
            - CD8: Loss & Avoidance: If you don't play for a month, your island gets covered in weeds and your favorite neighbors move away.
          - Builder's Takeaway: Use CD6 to pace the economy. By limiting how much a player can do in one day, you prevent burnout and extend the game's lifespan from weeks to years.
        - Engagement Score: Daily Habit Forming. It isn't played for 8 hours straight, but for 30 minutes every single day for years.
        - Builder’s Note: Use "Friction as a Feature." By forcing players to wait 24 real-world hours for a house upgrade, the reward feels significantly more valuable than if it were instant. Anticipation creates value.
          - Perfect example of appointment dynamics in games
      - Minecraft
        - Release Year: 2011
        - Consistent Players: ~212 Million Monthly Active Users (2025 estimate).
        - The Game Loop:
          - Gather: Punch trees, mine stone/coal.
          - Craft: Convert raw resources into tools to gather faster/deeper.
          - Build/Survive: Construct shelter to survive the night (scarcity) or automated farms (abundance).
          - Explore: Venture further for rarer resources (diamonds)
        - Dominant Drives: CD3 (Creativity) and CD7 (Curiosity)
          - The White Hat (Positive) Loop:
            - CD3: Empowerment of Creativity & Feedback: The ultimate sandbox. The economy is entirely player-driven (barter systems). The "LEGO" mechanic allows for infinite reconfiguration of the world.
            - CD2: Development & Accomplishment: Moving from wooden tools to stone, then iron, then diamond, then netherite. The power curve is steep and rewarding.
          - The Black Hat (Negative/Urgency) Loop:
            - CD7: Unpredictability & Curiosity: Exploring a cave is dangerous. You don't know if you will find diamonds or a Creeper. This slot-machine mechanic keeps mining exciting.
            - CD8: Loss & Avoidance: If you die in lava, you lose everything in your inventory. This high-stakes penalty gives value to the gear you earned via CD2.
          - Builder's Takeaway: CD7 is essential for "grinding." If mining stone was predictable and safe, it would be boring work. Add an element of danger/surprise to the labor.
        - Engagement Score: Infinite. Because the content is user-generated (mods, servers, builds), the game never officially "ends."
        - Builder’s Note: The "Lego Effect." If you let users build the world, they become emotionally attached to the server because they left a mark on it. Lesson: Give players tools, not just content.
      - Roblox
        - Release Year: 2006
        - Consistent Players: ~88 Million Daily Active Users (2025).
        - The Game Loop:
          - Socialize: Join a server where friends are hanging out.
          - Roleplay/Grind: Engage in simple loops (running a pizza shop, obstacle course).
          - Earn/Buy: Get currency (Robux) to buy skins/avatars.
          - Flex: Show off your unique avatar to peers.
        - Dominant Drives: CD5 (Social) and CD3 (Empowerment)
          - The White Hat (Positive) Loop:
            - CD5: Social Influence & Relatedness: This is the primary engine. If your friend has a cool avatar item (that costs money), you want it too. The economy is driven by envy and belonging.
            - CD3: Empowerment of Creativity: For developers, the ability to script games (Lua) and monetize them is pure CD3.
          - The Black Hat (Negative/Urgency) Loop:
            - CD6: Scarcity & Impatience: "Limited" items (UGC Limiteds) that are only sold for a short time or in small quantities create a frantic secondary market (reselling).
          - Builder's Takeaway: CD5 is the best way to monetize Gen Z. They care less about "power" (stats) and more about "expression" (skins/cosmetics) to signal status to peers.
        - Engagement Score: Viral/Social. Retention is driven by peer pressure (all your friends are playing Brookhaven, so you do too).
        - Builder’s Note: User-Generated Economy. Roblox wins because it allows players to be producers, not just consumers.
          - Lesson: If you can make your players rich (even in fake currency), they will work for you.
            - Carries over to UGC idea for Aurum - let players build virtual interconnected worlds with their own in-game economies (leveraging ownership and social drives)
      - World of Warcraft (MMORPGs)
        - Release Year: 2004
        - Consistent Players: ~7.25 Million Subscribers
        - The Game Loop:
          - Combat: Kill monsters/complete quests
          - Reward: Get Loot (Items) and XP
          - Power Up: Equip new items to kill stronger monsters
          - Social Raiding: Coordinate with 10-20 people to defeat complex bosses
        - Dominant Drives: CD2 (Accomplishment) and CD6 (Scarcity)
          - The White Hat (Positive) Loop:
            - CD2: Development & Accomplishment: The visual representation of progress (Level 1 -> Level 60). The economy supports this: you need gold to buy the gear to get the achievement.
            - CD1: Epic Meaning & Calling: "Horde vs. Alliance." Players feel they are contributing to a faction war, giving economic contributions (crafting war supplies) a higher purpose.
          - The Black Hat (Negative/Urgency) Loop:
            - CD6: Scarcity & Impatience: Rare loot drops (1% chance). The entire economy revolves around items that are hard to get.
            - CD7: Unpredictability: You raid for hours not knowing if the item will drop. This "Variable Reward Schedule" is identical to gambling.
          - Builder's Takeaway: Combine CD1 (Narrative) with CD2 (Stats). If players feel they are saving the world and getting rich doing it, the grind feels like a heroic duty.
        - Engagement Score: Sunk Cost Sticky. Players refuse to quit because they have invested thousands of hours into their character.
          - Leverages loss & avoidance drive
        - Builder’s Note: "The Skinner Box." Random rewards (RNG) are more addictive than predictable ones. If a boss drops the best sword every time, it's boring. If it drops it 5% of the time, players will grind the same boss for weeks.
          - Leverages unpredictability & curiosity drive
          - Seems to be lacking in effective scaffolding for new players - people join and become overwhelmed easily within the advanced ecosystem of the game

          - Comparison with Aurum
            - GravyStack is likely the closest direct-to-consumer (D2C) competitor in the "gamified financial literacy" space. It is a prominent player in the "Family Finance" sector.
            - 1. The Core Product Difference
              - GravyStack (The "Real" Bank): Their value proposition is "The Game is the Bank." They are a fintech banking platform (debit card + app) first. They gamify chores and allowance.
              - Aurum (The "Safe" Simulator): Your one-pager defines Aurum as an "immersive simulation" that provides a "risk-free environment".
                - Why this matters: GravyStack requires parents to move real money and open a bank account. Aurum allows users to practice before real money is at stake. This makes Aurum an easier sell to schools and risk-averse institutions.
            - 2. The Business Model Difference
              - GravyStack (B2C): They primarily sell subscriptions to parents (Direct-to-Consumer). This requires heavy marketing spend (high CAC) to convince parents to switch banking apps.
              - Aurum (B2B2C): You explicitly target "near-zero end-user CAC" by selling via "Institutional licensing (Schools, Banks)".
                - The Advantage: You aren't competing with GravyStack for Facebook ad space. You are getting deployed by a bank (perhaps even one that competes with GravyStack) to be their educational layer.
            - 3. The "Academic" Rigor
              - GravyStack: Focuses on "Home Economy" (chores, saving, spending). It is practical for younger kids (ages 6-12).
              - Aurum: Your messaging focuses on "complex financial decisions" and "academic rigor". This positions Aurum better for the High School / Young Adult / Corporate Wellness demographic ($53B market) where "chores" are no longer the primary lesson.
